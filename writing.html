    My experience starting with HTML, reflection on the Bruce Lawson articles “Why you should Choose HTML5 article over section” and “How A Screen Reader User Accesses The Web: A Smashing Video”, and the lecture on Internet histories

    Experience starting with HTML: starting with HTML: Starting to work with HTML was a fun experience. My favourite thing I learned this week was how easy it is to add media to a website. All I have to do is move the media to the folder that I am working in, and add it with the corresponding element, referencing the filename. This is possible in a similar way.

    Reflection on Readings: I completed the Bruce Larson reading, “Why you should Choose HTML5 article over section”. One thing that I found interesting from this reading (that was further emphasized in the technical lecture for the week), is the fact that, although two things might appear the same visually, if they are implemented in different ways, they can be functionally different. Examples include the <strong> and <b> tags, both of which make the text which they surround bold. Under the hood, however they function differently and can make differences in the discoverability and usability of my websites. Similarly, the reading also mentions that, wrapping an h1 element in a section element, can change the size of the h1 text. Visually, this is different, but functionally it is still the same. This would be a example of bad semantic markup.

    I also watched the video “How A Screen Reader User Accesses The Web: A Smashing Video”. This was eye-opening to me, because I've never thought of the fact that some people have to access the internet in a non-visual way. It makes sense that screen readers exist, as a manner of including people who would struggle to use technology without tools to make it possible. This video highlights the importance of using HTML properly in order to make websites easily comprehendible for everyone, despite context that might only be relevant for some people to understand what is going on. A good example of this, as mentioned in the “Why you should Choose HTML5 article over section”, is the fact that there is a quick summary of the web page at the top of the page. Without context, the writing from the summary could flow into the body of writing, which could be confusing for someone who cannot see the visual divider separating the summary from the start of the article.

    Reflection on Internet Histories: We also covered some of the history of the internet this week. I have many fond memories of playing browser games on the MiniClips website, a website that made significant usage of Flash.     Week 3

    Website development progress, reflection on the Mailloux piece titled “Convention and Context”, how it relates to the lecture we had (and how all that all ties into UI and UX), and thoughts on the supplementary lecture material that is available to us.

    Developments of website development: This week we were introduced to CSS, the tool that it is primarily responsible for styling our websites. Last week, while I was trying to create a wireframe, I struggled to create a vision for myself. This is largely because I was unsure of what would even be possible. Now that I know what is possible, I will use CSS as an opportunity to refine my website, into exactly what I want it to be (with the wireframe being a rough idea).

    The reading, lecture, and how it ties into UI and UX: Mailloux's Convention and Context had some insight that I think is valuable when considering how we choose to design our websites. Convention can be defined as commonly accepted practice within some context (Mailloux 1983: 399). In the context of UI and UX for web development, convention plays an important role for how we design websites for people to use. Feedback and responsiveness are conventional, because it tells the user that the actions that they are wanting to complete are understood. We make use of pre-existing website structures (even if we aren't necessarily aware of it) because they are familiar and make for an easy experience to a user who might not have ever visited our websites before. An example of this is search bars, which commonly appear in the header of websites. It would be unconventional to hide a search bar on the footer of a page because most people wouldn't think to look for it there.

    It is important to acknowledge when convention must be used in a sparing manner, as convention can limit innovation. While it helps to be familiar, it is equally important to think outside the box and push the boundaries to create new conventions to be used in the future/create experiences that are unique and memorable. The context of web development is changing (more companies have been using their websites as a primary source of traffic since COVID-19), and so consequently convention will change as well (Mailloux, 1983: 404). Conventions are tools that can help us as designers to create intuitive digital experiences for others.

    Supplementary material and why I think it's useful: I usually find that having multiple perspectives of the same work is beneficial. This course is no exception to that. I find that, if in the technical lectures, if there are concepts that I do not understand, it is sometimes worth going through the corresponding supplementary material. When an unfamiliar concept is given a fresh explanation, I find that I am more likely to absorb it the second time around. Although it is slightly more time consuming to approach understanding some topics in this way, I find that it is worth it in the end because my understanding of the topics on a whole is generally better.

    Week 5

    Website progress update, a discussion on colour theory, and a reflection on the practical lecture on the Document Object Model (DOM)

    Website progress: This week I spent some time considering the colour palette I want my website to have. While I understand that this is an opportunity to express myself in a unique way, I think it is important to remember that this website's purpose is to showcase a portfolio of our work to potential employers. With this in mind, I have chosen to keep my website image free where I can afford to, and use clean, professional colours. I have chosen to use dark blues, grey and orange, although this is subject to change.

    Colour Theory: This week I spent some time researching colour theory and how it applies to websites. Colour is an important factor for us to consider because it helps us communicate the state of the website to the user. It is useful to keep the colour wheel in mind when picking colours as this can be a useful tool in making effective choices. We can use complimentary colours (that sit on opposite sides of the colour wheel) to bring focus to specific elements. This is due to the high contrast between complimentary colours. I think I can use this to show the user that something they are hovering over is interactable. I think I will also primarily use a dark blue palette for the majority of my website to give it a harmonious and sophisticated look.

    Introduction to the Document Object Model (DOM): This week we were introduced to something called the Document Object Model. This is a useful tool that allows for real time manipulation of our websites. I can imagine that this is something that will be very useful to learn and understand well. I attempted the exercises but I struggled to complete them successfully. I think for the moment, I will be prioritizing the development of my website, but this is something that I want to master so I can use it effectively when it is needed in my website.

    Week 6

    Website development update, reflection on the lecture on IxD, and reflection on event handling

    Website progress: This week, I spent some time reconsidering the flow of my website. Originally, I had decided that my website would be one continuous page, with a static header that would direct the user to their desired section, but after some thinking I do not think that was the right idea. I have changed my design, to break my website into different pages that the user can navigate to. This way, the user is not overwhelmed with information immediately upon entering the site.

    While implementing the styling for my Weekly Dev Blog page, I encountered some unintentional styling that I have chosen to keep. The actual content of my weekly dev blog sits inside a section, inside another section, to visually separate each week's content. The styling I wanted to apply to the outer most section, applied to the inner one to, and the effect is that it looks like the user is being presented with the page from a journal, which I think is visually pleasing.

    IxD Process: This week we had a lecture on interaction design. It was eye opening to see how many things there are to take into consideration with regards to effective design for our website. I think that the idea of building a website in a way that makes it easy for someone who has not seen it before. On a smaller scale too, it makes sense to design sections on pages to function in similar ways. That way, for example, someone who is navigating from my Weekly Dev Blog page to my Design page is able to be familiar with how it works because it functions in a similar way.

    Event Handling: This week's practical lecture was a continuation of DOM manipulation stuff. We were exposed to event handling, which is how our website responds to user input. This is done through the use of something called “event listeners” which are used to detect user input. This is something that I can definitely see myself using in my own website (Instead of hard coding things in HTML, such as my Weekly Dev Blog content, it could be possible to use event handling to make some more complex ways to engage with the content), however this will have to happen at a later stage once I can familiarize myself more with how the DOM works better.

    Week 7

    Website development discussion, reflection on the IxD lecture, and how it applies to my website, and a reflection on the reading by Hassenzahl and Tractinsky titled “User experience - a research agenda”

    Website development: I have been taking the extended project brief into consideration with the continued development of my website. This process has forced me to reconsider the design of some sections of my website. In an effort to clean things up, I have decided to combine some sections (for the sake of reducing the number of options that the user is faced with at a time). This has forced me to rethink the flow of the website, and change my wireframes accordingly. Thankfully the actual implementation of my website is still fairly barebones, so it should not be a great effort to implement the changes I am wanting to make. Since the first assignment has little to no emphasis on aspects of JavaScript, I have prioritized becoming more confident in HTML and CSS. I attempted the DOM manipulation exercises, and although I found them challenging I am not worried at this stage, as I plan to find the time later on to sharpen these skills.

    IxD process lecture: I have taken the flow of my website into consideration. I intend to have a header that stays permanently visible (in desktop mode, at least), as a means for easy navigation for the user. I am attempting to make each of my pages internally familiar, as this will help the user to get used to the format of my website quickly, and I will use external familiarity elements so that the user is given a seamless experience when viewing my website for the first time.

    We want the users of our website to think about the decisions they are making as little as possible, hence the importance of Hick’s Law. I have been trying to find ways to condense the number of decisions that the user can be faced with at one time. In terms of navigation, I have taken this into account by combining similar sections together. This has been done so that initial navigation is less intimidating, and the user is able to increase the specificity of their navigation as they need to.

    I intend to use emphasis and dominance to show the user information that they have selected. This will be seen, on the header giving the user a visual indication of which page of the website they are currently on, and on the page in general if they are hovering over/close to something that they can interact with. I will change size and shape, to effectively communicate potential and completed actions.

    Weekly reading reflection: We cannot design in a way that expects the audience to achieve the exact same experience through our design, but we can design in a way that ensures that the audience can use the design to arrive at an individual experience (Hassenzahl & Tractinksy 2006: 95). UX takes the experience of the user into account while HCI is primarily focused on the functionality of the interface. As we take into account the UX aspects of our websites, it is important to consider both the ease of use and effectiveness of our website, as well as the experiences for the user that come as a consequence of engaging with our website.
