  • Week 1

    Experience setting up Git, reflection on Bush’s “As We May Think” and Borges’ “The Library of Babel”, and goals for the course

    Setting up GitHub: I have set up and briefly used GitHub prior to this course. I have taken the opportunity to review the website, as after creating an account, I have made use of GitHub desktop on a more frequent basis than the actual website. I noticed some beta features on the website, that are not present on the desktop software such as a “trending repositories” and a “recommended for you” section, which struck me as particularly interesting, because to me these seem like features on a social media platform, rather than a website that is used more so in a working/studying context.

    Readings: “As we may think” is a piece that is set more in reality, touching on the development of technology, primarily the evolution of technology responsible for creating and storing information. It speaks of the “memex”, a tool that closely mirrors some functionality that the internet also has (mainly that it served as a catalogue for pre-existing information). I think that the internet is becoming steadily more essential in our daily lives, as the retrieval of information is necessary for many of our daily tasks. It is also worth noting, however, that the longer the internet stays relevant, the more information is stockpiled in the same place. This can be seen as both a good thing and a bad thing. The collection and retrieval of information in one place can be seen as quite convenient. In contrast, with the amount of information on the internet, the reliability thereof becomes difficult to gauge.

    “The Library of Babel” was a fictional metaphorical piece, which I interpreted as a warning of overabundance of information (like the internet is today). I think that it is true that we are oversaturated with information – similar to the previous reading. Perhaps it is part of our responsibility as future web developers to present information to people in a way that is not overwhelming?

    Goals: One of my goals for this course is to try to work consistently through the course material. I would also like to further my technical ability in coding, in order to make functional websites, and understand the theoretical elements that make websites appealing, ultimately combining the two. I'm also interested in learning what our responsibilities are regarding what we put out onto the internet.

    Week 2

    My experience starting with HTML, reflection on the Bruce Lawson articles “Why you should Choose HTML5 article over section” and “How A Screen Reader User Accesses The Web: A Smashing Video”, and the lecture on Internet histories

    Experience starting with HTML: starting with HTML: Starting to work with HTML was a fun experience. My favourite thing I learned this week was how easy it is to add media to a website. All I have to do is move the media to the folder that I am working in, and add it with the corresponding element, referencing the filename. This will be important for me to know, as I intend to to add pictures and videos to my websites. I would also like to make navigating around and between pages of the website quick and easy. This is possible in a similar way.

    Reflection on Readings: I completed the Bruce Larson reading, “Why you should Choose HTML5 article over section”. One thing that I found interesting from this reading (that was further emphasized in the technical lecture for the week), is the fact that, although two things might appear the same visually, if they are implemented in different ways, they can be functionally different. Examples include the <strong> and <b> tags, both of which make the text which they surround bold. Under the hood, however they function differently and can make differences in the discoverability and usability of my websites. Similarly, the reading also mentions that, wrapping an h1 element in a section element, can change the size of the h1 text. Visually, this is different, but functionally it is still the same. This would be a example of bad semantic markup.

    I also watched the video “How A Screen Reader User Accesses The Web: A Smashing Video”. This was eye-opening to me, because I’ve never thought of the fact that some people have to access the internet in a non-visual way. It makes sense that screen readers exist, as a manner of including people who would struggle to use technology without tools to make it possible. This video highlights the importance of using HTML properly in order to make websites easily comprehendible for everyone, despite context that might only be relevant for some people to understand what is going on. A good example of this, as mentioned in the “Why you should Choose HTML5 article over section”, is the fact that there is a quick summary of the web page at the top of the page. Without context, the writing from the summary could flow into the body of writing, which could be confusing for someone who cannot see the visual divider separating the summary from the start of the article.

    Reflection on Internet Histories: We also covered some of the history of the internet this week. I have many fond memories of playing browser games on the MiniClips website, a website that made significant usage of Flash. It has also been interesting to see how the internet has been monopolized throughout it’s life cycle.
